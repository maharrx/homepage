Projects

Sofia

As a part of a larger project that aimed to improve the quality of life of people living with mental illness, three distinct empirical studies were carried out to explore conversational agent's potential in supporting mental health and wellbeing:

  1. 3987 online reviews of 719 Alexa health and fitness skills were analyzed using an automated text analysis approach (Structured Topic Modeling) with an initial categorization of the agents to understand the landscape of the health related conversational agents and everyday users’ perspectives on such technology.
  2. Built an agent prototype to employ a health questionnaire using finite-state-based and free-form conversational interaction design models, conducted a lab study to explore the feasibility of the technology for gathering mental health self-reports, and analyzed the impact of conversational designs on users' self-reporting behaviors and experiences.
  3. Developed an agent that enabled users to maintain a verbal daily diary log and respond to a health questionnaire fortnightly, conducted a four-week field study involving 20 individuals with mental illness, and interviewed them to assess the agent's ecological validity as well as to inform future design of the agents to support mental health self-reports by analyzing users' perceived experiences.

m CARDIA

Following the user-centered design principles, this project designed and developed a prototype of a heart rhythm monitoring system called mCardia which included an android app for the patients and a web app for the clinicians. The mobile app contextually visualized the cardiovascular data (e.g., ECG signals, Heart Rate, MET level and step counts) collected through a Holter device and allowd patients to self-reports (e.g., physical activity, stress level and symptoms). A web application was developed for clinicians to enable them to analyze and annotate the data for timely intervention and prevention of serious conditions such as atrial fibrillation and stroke. The study resulted in the technical implementation of the system design, followed by a feasibility study. The study involved the following three steps:

  1. Requirements gathered from both patients and clinicians through series of interviews, co-design workshops, and field visits to the cardiology department in a large Danish hospital.
  2. Considering different and sometimes conflicting requirements from the stakeholders, prototypes of patient mobile app and the web app for the clinicians were designed through multiple iterations.
  3. A two-week feasibility study involving 24 patients, and a workshop with 4 clinicians including two cardiologists, a pacemaker doctor and a research nurse was conducted to evaluate the system.
